Present Progressive (Present Continuous)

The Present Progressive tense in Spanish (Presente Continuo) is used to express actions that are happening at the moment of speaking. It is formed by combining a conjugated form of the verb estar with the gerund (gerundio) of the main verb.

When to Use the Present Progressive

  • To describe actions in progress right now: "I am studying" (Estoy estudiando)
  • To talk about temporary activities: "She is living in Madrid" (Está viviendo en Madrid)
  • To describe actions happening around the present moment: "We are working on a project" (Estamos trabajando en un proyecto)

Forming the Gerund

Verb Type Stem Ending Example
-AR verbs Remove -ar -ando hablar → hablando (speaking)
-ER verbs Remove -er -iendo comer → comiendo (eating)
-IR verbs Remove -ir -iendo vivir → viviendo (living)

Irregular Gerunds

There are three main types of irregularities:

1. Stem-changing -IR verbs: When the stem contains 'e' or 'o', they often change to 'i' or 'u'

Infinitive Gerund
dormir (to sleep) durmiendo (sleeping)
morir (to die) muriendo (dying)
pedir (to ask for) pidiendo (asking for)
servir (to serve) sirviendo (serving)
poder (to be able) pudiendo (being able)
decir (to say) diciendo (saying)
venir (to come) viniendo (coming)

2. Verbs ending in -er or -ir with stems ending in a vowel: Add -yendo instead of -iendo

Infinitive Gerund
leer (to read) leyendo (reading)
oír (to hear) oyendo (hearing)
creer (to believe) creyendo (believing)
traer (to bring) trayendo (bringing)

3. Other irregular gerunds:

Infinitive Gerund
ir (to go) yendo (going)

Complete Present Progressive Construction

To form the Present Progressive, combine a conjugated form of estar with the gerund:

Subject Estar Example with hablar
yo estoy Estoy hablando (I am speaking)
tú estás Estás hablando (You are speaking)
él/ella/usted está Está hablando (He/she is speaking)
nosotros/as estamos Estamos hablando (We are speaking)
vosotros/as estáis Estáis hablando (You all are speaking)
ellos/ellas/ustedes están Están hablando (They are speaking)

Examples:

¿Qué estás haciendo? Estoy cocinando la cena. (What are you doing? I am cooking dinner.)

Los niños están jugando en el parque. (The children are playing in the park.)

Estamos aprendiendo español este año. (We are learning Spanish this year.)

Note: In Spanish, the Present Simple can sometimes be used where English would use the Present Progressive, especially with temporary situations: "Vivo en Madrid ahora" can mean "I am living in Madrid now."

Comparisons

Spanish uses specific structures to make comparisons between people, things, or actions.

Comparisons of Inequality

1. To express "more than": más + adjective/adverb/noun + que

Juan es más alto que Pedro. (Juan is taller than Pedro.)

Ella habla más rápido que yo. (She speaks faster than me.)

Tengo más libros que mi hermana. (I have more books than my sister.)

2. To express "less than": menos + adjective/adverb/noun + que

Este hotel es menos caro que el otro. (This hotel is less expensive than the other one.)

Estudio menos horas que mi compañero. (I study fewer hours than my classmate.)

Hay menos gente que ayer. (There are fewer people than yesterday.)

Comparisons of Equality

1. With adjectives and adverbs: tan + adjective/adverb + como

Mi casa es tan grande como la tuya. (My house is as big as yours.)

Él no corre tan rápido como tú. (He doesn't run as fast as you.)

2. With nouns: tanto/a/os/as + noun + como

Tengo tantos amigos como tú. (I have as many friends as you.)

No hay tantas sillas como personas. (There aren't as many chairs as people.)

Note: "Tanto" must agree in gender and number with the noun it modifies:

- tanto (masculine singular)

- tanta (feminine singular)

- tantos (masculine plural)

- tantas (feminine plural)

Irregular Comparatives

Some adjectives have irregular comparative forms:

Adjective Comparative Form Example
bueno (good) mejor (better) Esta película es mejor que la otra. (This movie is better than the other one.)
malo (bad) peor (worse) El tráfico es peor que ayer. (The traffic is worse than yesterday.)
grande (big) mayor (bigger/older)* Mi hermano es mayor que yo. (My brother is older than me.)
pequeño (small) menor (smaller/younger)* Su hija es menor que la mía. (Her daughter is younger than mine.)

* Note: "Mayor" and "menor" typically refer to age when used with people. For size, "más grande" and "más pequeño" are often used.

Superlatives

To express the highest degree of a quality ("the most", "the least"), use:

1. Relative superlatives: el/la/los/las + más/menos + adjective + de

Es el edificio más alto de la ciudad. (It's the tallest building in the city.)

Son los días menos fríos del invierno. (They're the least cold days of winter.)

2. Irregular superlatives:

bueno → el mejor (the best)

malo → el peor (the worst)

grande → el mayor (the oldest/biggest)

pequeño → el menor (the youngest/smallest)

Imperativo Afirmativo (Affirmative Imperative)

The Affirmative Imperative is used to give direct commands, instructions, advice, or invitations. It has different forms depending on who you're addressing.

Regular Forms

Subject -AR verbs (hablar) -ER verbs (comer) -IR verbs (vivir)
tú habla come vive
usted hable coma viva
nosotros/as hablemos comamos vivamos
vosotros/as hablad comed vivid
ustedes hablen coman vivan

Note: The tú form is the same as the third-person singular present indicative.

The vosotros form is created by removing the final -r from the infinitive and adding -d.

Irregular Forms

Several common verbs have irregular imperative forms, especially in the tú form:

Verb tú usted nosotros vosotros ustedes
decir (to say) di diga digamos decid digan
hacer (to do) haz haga hagamos haced hagan
ir (to go) ve vaya vayamos id vayan
poner (to put) pon ponga pongamos poned pongan
salir (to leave) sal salga salgamos salid salgan
ser (to be) sé sea seamos sed sean
tener (to have) ten tenga tengamos tened tengan
venir (to come) ven venga vengamos venid vengan

Using the Imperative with Pronouns

When object pronouns are used with affirmative commands, they are attached to the end of the imperative form:

Dímelo. (Tell it to me.)

Cómpraselo. (Buy it for him/her.)

Póntelo. (Put it on yourself.)

Note: When adding pronouns to the imperative form, an accent mark is often needed to maintain the original stress:

Habla (Speak) → Háblame (Speak to me)

Come (Eat) → Cómelo (Eat it)

More examples:

Habla más despacio, por favor. (Speak more slowly, please.)

Vengan a las ocho. (Come at eight o'clock.)

Vamos a la playa. (Let's go to the beach.)

Escuchad con atención. (Listen carefully.)

Cierra la puerta. (Close the door.)

Direct Object Pronouns

Direct object pronouns replace direct objects (the person or thing directly receiving the action of the verb) to avoid repetition. They answer the question "what?" or "whom?"

Forms of Direct Object Pronouns

Person Singular Plural
First Person me (me) nos (us)
Second Person te (you) os (you all)
Third Person Masculine lo (him, it) los (them)
Third Person Feminine la (her, it) las (them)

Note: Unlike in English, Spanish direct object pronouns change based on gender. "Lo" and "los" are used for masculine nouns, while "la" and "las" are used for feminine nouns.

Position of Direct Object Pronouns

1. Before conjugated verbs:

Yo lo compro. (I buy it.)

La veo todos los días. (I see her every day.)

2. Attached to infinitives:

Quiero comprarlo. (I want to buy it.)

Necesito verla. (I need to see her.)

3. Attached to gerunds:

Estoy comprandolo. (I am buying it.)

Sigo buscandolas. (I keep looking for them.)

4. Attached to affirmative commands:

Compralo. (Buy it.)

Llamame. (Call me.)

5. Before negative commands:

No lo compres. (Don't buy it.)

No me llames. (Don't call me.)

Special Cases and Usage

1. Placement with two-verb constructions:

With verbs like "ir a + infinitive," "tener que + infinitive," etc., the direct object pronoun can go either before the conjugated verb or attached to the infinitive:

Lo voy a comprar. = Voy a comprarlo. (I am going to buy it.)

La tienes que llamar. = Tienes que llamarla. (You have to call her.)

2. Using with indirect object pronouns:

When both direct and indirect object pronouns are used together, the indirect object pronoun comes first:

Te lo doy. (I give it to you.)

Me la envía. (He/she sends it to me.)

3. Accent marks with attached pronouns:

When attaching pronouns to verbs, an accent mark may be needed to maintain the original stress:

Dar + lo = Darlo (no accent needed)

Da + me + lo = Dámelo (accent needed)

More examples:

¿Dónde está mi libro? Lo dejé en la mesa. (Where is my book? I left it on the table.)

¿Conoces a María? Sí, la conozco desde hace años. (Do you know María? Yes, I've known her for years.)

Necesito mis llaves. No puedo encontrarlas. (I need my keys. I can't find them.)

¿Vas a llamar a tus padres? Sí, voy a llamarlos esta noche. (Are you going to call your parents? Yes, I'm going to call them tonight.)
