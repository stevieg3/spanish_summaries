Pretérito Imperfecto (Imperfect Past)

The Pretérito Imperfecto is used to describe habitual or repeated actions in the past, ongoing actions without emphasizing their completion, past descriptions, and to tell time or age in the past.

When to Use the Imperfect

  • Habitual actions in the past: "I used to walk every day" (Yo caminaba todos los días)
  • Ongoing background actions: "It was raining when I arrived" (Llovía cuando llegué)
  • Descriptions of past situations: "The house was large" (La casa era grande)
  • Telling time in the past: "It was three o'clock" (Eran las tres)
  • Stating age in the past: "I was 10 years old" (Yo tenía diez años)

Regular Conjugations

Subject -AR verbs (hablar) -ER verbs (comer) -IR verbs (vivir)
yo hablaba comía vivía
tú hablabas comías vivías
él/ella/usted hablaba comía vivía
nosotros/as hablábamos comíamos vivíamos
vosotros/as hablabais comíais vivíais
ellos/ellas/ustedes hablaban comían vivían

Note: -ER and -IR verbs share the same endings in the imperfect tense.

Irregular Verbs in the Imperfect

There are only three irregular verbs in the imperfect tense:

Subject ser (to be) ir (to go) ver (to see)
yo era iba veía
tú eras ibas veías
él/ella/usted era iba veía
nosotros/as éramos íbamos veíamos
vosotros/as erais ibais veíais
ellos/ellas/ustedes eran iban veían

Examples:

Cuando era niño, jugaba al fútbol todos los días. (When I was a child, I used to play soccer every day.)

Mientras yo estudiaba, mi hermano veía la televisión. (While I was studying, my brother was watching television.)

Siempre íbamos a la playa en verano. (We always used to go to the beach in summer.)

Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns replace nouns and show ownership. In Spanish, they must agree in gender and number with the noun they replace (not the owner).

Forms of Possessive Pronouns

Owner Masculine Singular Feminine Singular Masculine Plural Feminine Plural
mine (yo) el mío la mía los míos las mías
yours (tú) el tuyo la tuya los tuyos las tuyas
his/hers/its/yours (formal) el suyo la suya los suyos las suyas
ours el nuestro la nuestra los nuestros las nuestras
yours (plural/vosotros) el vuestro la vuestra los vuestros las vuestras
theirs/yours (formal plural) el suyo la suya los suyos las suyas

Note: The definite article (el, la, los, las) is usually required before the possessive pronoun.

Usage

Spanish possessive pronouns replace a noun that belongs to someone:

Examples:

Mi coche es rojo y el tuyo es azul. (My car is red and yours is blue.)

Esta casa es la mía. (This house is mine.)

Sus problemas son más graves que los nuestros. (Their problems are more serious than ours.)

Disambiguation with "suyo"

Since "suyo" can mean "his," "hers," "its," "yours (formal)," or "theirs," Spanish often uses clarifying phrases to avoid ambiguity:

El suyo de Juan (His - Juan's)

El suyo de usted (Yours - formal)

El suyo de ellos (Theirs)

Indirect Object Pronouns

Indirect object pronouns indicate to whom or for whom an action is performed. They answer the question "to whom?" or "for whom?"

Forms of Indirect Object Pronouns

Person Singular Plural
First Person me (to/for me) nos (to/for us)
Second Person te (to/for you) os (to/for you all)
Third Person le (to/for him/her/it/you formal) les (to/for them/you all formal)

Position of Indirect Object Pronouns

1. Before conjugated verbs:

Yo te compro un regalo. (I buy you a gift.)

Ella nos envía cartas. (She sends us letters.)

2. Attached to infinitives:

Quiero decirte algo. (I want to tell you something.)

Vamos a comprarle un regalo. (We are going to buy him/her a gift.)

3. Attached to gerunds:

Estoy diciendote la verdad. (I am telling you the truth.)

Estamos enviándoles dinero. (We are sending them money.)

4. Attached to positive commands:

Díme la verdad. (Tell me the truth.)

Comprales algo. (Buy them something.)

5. Before negative commands:

No me digas mentiras. (Don't tell me lies.)

No le compres nada. (Don't buy him/her anything.)

Using with Direct Object Pronouns

When both direct and indirect object pronouns are used together, the indirect object pronoun always comes first:

Te lo doy. (I give it to you.)

Nos la envía. (He/she sends it to us.)

Note: When "le" or "les" is followed by "lo," "la," "los," or "las," "le" changes to "se" to avoid the awkward sound of two l's:

Le + lo = Se lo (NOT le lo)

Example: Se lo doy a Juan. (I give it to Juan.)

Clarifying with Redundant Indirect Objects

Since "le" and "les" can refer to multiple people, Spanish often adds a clarifying phrase:

Le compré un regalo a mi madre. (I bought my mother a gift.)

Les escribo cartas a mis abuelos. (I write letters to my grandparents.)
