Pretérito Perfecto (Present Perfect)

The Pretérito Perfecto (also called Present Perfect in English) is used to express actions that have happened in a period of time that includes the present or have a connection to the present moment. It is formed by using the present tense of the auxiliary verb haber + the past participle (participio) of the main verb.

When to Use the Pretérito Perfecto

  • To talk about actions that have occurred in a time period that is not finished yet: "I have studied today" (He estudiado hoy)
  • To describe experiences that have happened at some indefinite time in the past: "I have visited Paris" (He visitado París)
  • To express actions that started in the past but continue or have effects in the present: "I have lived here for 5 years" (He vivido aquí durante 5 años)
  • To talk about recent actions with relevance to the present: "I have just eaten" (Acabo de comer)

Conjugation of Haber in Present Tense

Subject Haber (auxiliary verb)
yo he
tú has
él/ella/usted ha
nosotros/as hemos
vosotros/as habéis
ellos/ellas/ustedes han

Forming the Past Participle

Verb Type Ending Example
-AR verbs -ar + ado hablar → hablado (spoken)
-ER verbs -er + ido comer → comido (eaten)
-IR verbs -ir + ido vivir → vivido (lived)

Irregular Past Participles

Many common Spanish verbs have irregular past participles:

Infinitive Past Participle
abrir (to open) abierto
decir (to say) dicho
escribir (to write) escrito
hacer (to do/make) hecho
morir (to die) muerto
poner (to put) puesto
romper (to break) roto
ver (to see) visto
volver (to return) vuelto

Common Time Expressions with Pretérito Perfecto

  • hoy (today)
  • esta mañana/tarde/noche/semana/mes/año (this morning/afternoon/evening/week/month/year)
  • alguna vez (ever)
  • nunca (never)
  • ya (already)
  • todavía no (not yet)
  • últimamente (lately)
  • en mi vida (in my life)
  • hasta ahora (until now)

Examples:

¿Has viajado a España alguna vez? (Have you ever traveled to Spain?)

He estudiado mucho esta semana. (I have studied a lot this week.)

¿Habéis terminado ya la tarea? (Have you all finished the homework already?)

Mi hermano ha visto esa película tres veces. (My brother has seen that movie three times.)

Todavía no hemos hecho las compras. (We haven't done the shopping yet.)

Note: In Spain, the Pretérito Perfecto is used more frequently than in Latin America, where the Pretérito Indefinido is often preferred even for recent past actions.

Comparison between Pretérito Perfecto and Pretérito Indefinido

Although both the Pretérito Perfecto and Pretérito Indefinido refer to actions completed in the past, they are used in different contexts and have different implications.

Key Differences

Pretérito Perfecto (Present Perfect) Pretérito Indefinido (Simple Past)
Connects past actions to the present Refers to completed actions with no connection to the present
Used for actions in time periods that include the present Used for actions in time periods that are completely finished
Often used with present time markers Often used with specific past time markers
Formation: haber (present) + past participle Formation: simple verb form with specific endings

Time Expressions Comparison

Pretérito Perfecto Pretérito Indefinido
hoy (today) ayer (yesterday)
esta mañana/tarde/semana (this morning/afternoon/week) la semana pasada (last week)
este mes/año (this month/year) el mes/año pasado (last month/year)
alguna vez (ever) hace dos días/años (two days/years ago)
hasta ahora (until now) anoche (last night)
últimamente (lately) entonces (then)
nunca (never - when referring to "up until now") una vez (once - specific occurrence)

Contrasting Examples:

He visitado Madrid este año. (I have visited Madrid this year.) - The year is not over yet.

Visité Madrid el año pasado. (I visited Madrid last year.) - The year is completely finished.

¿Has comido algo hoy? (Have you eaten anything today?) - Today is not over.

¿Comiste algo ayer? (Did you eat anything yesterday?) - Yesterday is completely over.

He estado muy ocupado últimamente. (I have been very busy lately.) - Continuing situation.

Estuve muy ocupado la semana pasada. (I was very busy last week.) - Completed situation.

Note: Regional differences exist in the use of these tenses. In Spain, the distinction between these tenses is maintained more strictly, while in parts of Latin America (especially Mexico, Colombia, and Argentina), the Pretérito Indefinido is often used in contexts where Spain would use the Pretérito Perfecto.

How to Choose Between These Tenses

  • Use Pretérito Perfecto when:
    • The time period referred to includes the present
    • The action has relevance or connection to the present
    • You are talking about experiences up to the present moment
    • The action has just happened
  • Use Pretérito Indefinido when:
    • The action occurred at a specific point in the past
    • The time period is completely over
    • The action is seen as completed with no connection to the present
    • You are narrating a sequence of events in the past

Obligation and Wishes Expressions

Spanish has several ways to express obligation, necessity, wishes and desires using verb constructions followed by an infinitive.

Querer + Infinitivo (to want to)

"Querer" followed by an infinitive expresses wishes or desires about actions.

Subject Present Tense
yo quiero + infinitive
tú quieres + infinitive
él/ella/usted quiere + infinitive
nosotros/as queremos + infinitive
vosotros/as queréis + infinitive
ellos/ellas/ustedes quieren + infinitive

Examples:

Quiero aprender español. (I want to learn Spanish.)

¿Quieres ir al cine esta noche? (Do you want to go to the movies tonight?)

Ella quiere viajar por Europa este verano. (She wants to travel around Europe this summer.)

Necesitar + Infinitivo (to need to)

"Necesitar" followed by an infinitive expresses necessity or need to do something.

Subject Present Tense
yo necesito + infinitive
tú necesitas + infinitive
él/ella/usted necesita + infinitive
nosotros/as necesitamos + infinitive
vosotros/as necesitáis + infinitive
ellos/ellas/ustedes necesitan + infinitive

Examples:

Necesito dormir más. (I need to sleep more.)

Necesitamos comprar comida para la cena. (We need to buy food for dinner.)

¿Necesitas ayuda con tu tarea? (Do you need help with your homework?)

Tener que + Infinitivo (to have to)

"Tener que" followed by an infinitive expresses obligation or necessity, similar to "must" or "have to" in English. It is stronger than "necesitar" and often implies external obligation.

Subject Present Tense
yo tengo que + infinitive
tú tienes que + infinitive
él/ella/usted tiene que + infinitive
nosotros/as tenemos que + infinitive
vosotros/as tenéis que + infinitive
ellos/ellas/ustedes tienen que + infinitive

Examples:

Tengo que estudiar para el examen. (I have to study for the exam.)

Tienes que llamar a tu madre. (You have to call your mother.)

Los estudiantes tienen que entregar el trabajo mañana. (The students have to submit the assignment tomorrow.)

Hay que + Infinitivo (one must/it is necessary to)

"Hay que" followed by an infinitive expresses general or impersonal obligation. It translates as "one must" or "it is necessary to" and does not change form since it is impersonal.

Examples:

Hay que estudiar para aprobar. (One must study to pass.)

Hay que respetar las reglas. (It is necessary to respect the rules.)

Hay que lavar los platos después de comer. (One must wash the dishes after eating.)

Note: "Hay que" is impersonal and doesn't change form regardless of who is speaking or being addressed. It's used for general statements about what should be done, not directed at any specific person.

Comparison of Obligation Expressions

Expression Level of Obligation Usage
Querer + infinitivo No obligation (desire) Expresses personal wishes or desires
Necesitar + infinitivo Mild obligation (need) Expresses personal necessity
Tener que + infinitivo Strong obligation Expresses personal obligation or necessity
Hay que + infinitivo General obligation Expresses impersonal or general obligation

Comparative Examples:

Quiero leer este libro. (I want to read this book.) - Personal desire

Necesito leer este libro. (I need to read this book.) - Personal necessity

Tengo que leer este libro para la clase. (I have to read this book for class.) - Personal obligation

Hay que leer mucho para aprender un idioma. (One must read a lot to learn a language.) - General advice
