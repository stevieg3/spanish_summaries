Time Expressions for Ongoing Actions

Spanish has several expressions to describe how long an action has been happening. These time expressions are essential for talking about ongoing situations that began in the past and continue into the present.

Desde hace + time period

Used to express "for" or "since" when referring to a period of time from the past until now.

Estudio español desde hace tres años. (I have been studying Spanish for three years.)

Vivo en Madrid desde hace seis meses. (I have been living in Madrid for six months.)

Trabajo en esta empresa desde hace mucho tiempo. (I have been working at this company for a long time.)

Desde que + clause

Used to express "since" when referring to a specific point in time or event that started the action.

Soy vegetariano desde que vi un documental sobre la industria cárnica. (I've been a vegetarian since I watched a documentary about the meat industry.)

No he vuelto a Francia desde que terminé la universidad. (I haven't returned to France since I finished university.)

Estamos juntos desde que nos conocimos en 2015. (We've been together since we met in 2015.)

Hace + time period + que

An alternative structure to express "for" or "how long" an action has been happening.

Hace tres años que estudio español. (I have been studying Spanish for three years.)

Hace seis meses que vivo en Madrid. (I have been living in Madrid for six months.)

¿Hace cuánto tiempo que trabajas aquí? (How long have you been working here?)

Llevar + time period + gerund

This construction emphasizes the duration of an ongoing action.

Llevo tres años estudiando español. (I have been studying Spanish for three years.)

Llevo seis meses viviendo en Madrid. (I have been living in Madrid for six months.)

¿Cuánto tiempo llevas trabajando aquí? (How long have you been working here?)

Note: These expressions are typically used with the present tense in Spanish, even though they often translate to present perfect continuous in English.

When referring to the past, you can use these expressions with the imperfect tense:

Llevaba tres meses viviendo allí cuando ocurrió el terremoto. (I had been living there for three months when the earthquake happened.)

Contrast between Pretérito Imperfecto and Pretérito Indefinido

Understanding when to use the Pretérito Imperfecto versus the Pretérito Indefinido is one of the most challenging aspects of Spanish grammar for English speakers. Each tense has specific uses and conveys different perspectives on past actions.

Pretérito Imperfecto (Imperfect)

  • Describes ongoing, habitual, or repeated actions in the past
  • Provides background information or sets the scene
  • Describes states of being, conditions, or feelings in the past
  • Tells what was happening or what used to happen
  • Has no defined endpoint; focuses on the process rather than completion

Pretérito Indefinido (Preterite)

  • Expresses completed actions at a specific time in the past
  • Narrates events in sequence
  • Focuses on actions with clear beginnings and endings
  • Tells what happened or what someone did
  • Has a defined endpoint; focuses on completion rather than process

Comparing the Two Tenses

Examples showing contrast:

Cuando era niño, vivía en Barcelona. (When I was a child, I used to live in Barcelona.) [Imperfecto: describes a state and a habitual situation]

Viví en Barcelona durante tres años y luego me mudé a Madrid. (I lived in Barcelona for three years and then moved to Madrid.) [Indefinido: completed actions with defined time periods]

Mientras dormía, alguien entró en la casa. (While I was sleeping, someone entered the house.) [Imperfecto for background action + Indefinido for interrupting action]

Ayer estaba muy cansado, pero aun así salí con mis amigos. (Yesterday I was very tired, but I still went out with my friends.) [Imperfecto for state + Indefinido for action]

Note: Some verbs change meaning depending on whether they are used in the Imperfecto or Indefinido:

Conocía a Juan. (I knew Juan. / I was acquainted with Juan.)

Conocí a Juan. (I met Juan. / I got to know Juan.)

Sabía la respuesta. (I knew the answer.)

Supe la respuesta. (I found out the answer.)

Podía nadar. (I could swim. / I was able to swim.)

Pude nadar. (I managed to swim. / I was able to swim [on a specific occasion].)

Imperfecto estar + gerundio (Past Continuous)

The past continuous tense in Spanish is formed with the imperfect tense of "estar" and the gerund (present participle) of the main verb. It's used to describe actions that were in progress at a specific moment in the past.

Formation

Imperfect of estar + gerund

Subject Estar (Imperfect) + Gerund Example with hablar
yo estaba + gerund estaba hablando (I was speaking)
tú estabas + gerund estabas hablando (you were speaking)
él/ella/usted estaba + gerund estaba hablando (he/she was speaking)
nosotros/as estábamos + gerund estábamos hablando (we were speaking)
vosotros/as estabais + gerund estabais hablando (you all were speaking)
ellos/ellas/ustedes estaban + gerund estaban hablando (they were speaking)

Remember: To form the gerund, follow these patterns:

-AR verbs: stem + ando (hablar → hablando)

-ER/-IR verbs: stem + iendo (comer → comiendo, vivir → viviendo)

Some verbs have irregular gerunds (dormir → durmiendo, pedir → pidiendo, etc.)

Uses of Imperfecto estar + gerundio

  • To describe actions that were in progress at a specific moment in the past
  • To describe an action that was interrupted by another action (with Indefinido)
  • To provide context or background to a narrative
  • To express what someone was doing during a period of time

Examples:

A las ocho de la mañana, estaba durmiendo. (At eight in the morning, I was sleeping.)

Mientras estábamos cenando, sonó el teléfono. (While we were having dinner, the telephone rang.)

¿Qué estabas haciendo cuando ocurrió el accidente? (What were you doing when the accident happened?)

Los niños estaban jugando en el parque cuando comenzó a llover. (The children were playing in the park when it started to rain.)

Note: The difference between simple Imperfecto and "estar + gerundio" is similar to the difference in English between "I walked" (yo caminaba) and "I was walking" (yo estaba caminando). The "estar + gerundio" form emphasizes the ongoing nature of the action more clearly.

Futuro Simple (Simple Future Tense)

The Futuro Simple (Simple Future) is used to express actions that will happen in the future, make predictions, or express probability.

Regular Conjugations

The Simple Future tense is formed by adding specific endings to the infinitive form of the verb (unlike many other Spanish tenses, which require removing the infinitive ending first).

Subject -AR/-ER/-IR verbs Example (hablar) Example (comer) Example (vivir)
yo infinitive + é hablaré comeré viviré
tú infinitive + ás hablarás comerás vivirás
él/ella/usted infinitive + á hablará comerá vivirá
nosotros/as infinitive + emos hablaremos comeremos viviremos
vosotros/as infinitive + éis hablaréis comeréis viviréis
ellos/ellas/ustedes infinitive + án hablarán comerán vivirán

Note: All verbs (-AR, -ER, and -IR) use the same endings in the future tense. The endings are added to the infinitive form of the verb without removing the infinitive ending.

Irregular Stems

Some common verbs have irregular stems in the future tense, but they still use the regular future tense endings.

Infinitive Irregular Stem Example (yo form)
decir (to say) dir- diré
hacer (to do/make) har- haré
haber (to have, auxiliary) habr- habré
poder (to be able) podr- podré
poner (to put) pondr- pondré
querer (to want) querr- querré
saber (to know) sabr- sabré
salir (to leave) saldr- saldré
tener (to have) tendr- tendré
venir (to come) vendr- vendré

Uses of the Futuro Simple

  • To talk about future actions or events: Mañana iré al cine. (Tomorrow I will go to the movies.)
  • To make predictions: Creo que lloverá mañana. (I think it will rain tomorrow.)
  • To express probability in the present: Serán las ocho. (It must be around eight o'clock.)
  • To express commands or wishes: Harás lo que te digo. (You will do what I tell you.)
  • To express intentions: Te llamaré esta noche. (I will call you tonight.)

Examples:

El año que viene estudiaré en España. (Next year I will study in Spain.)

¿Vendrás a mi fiesta el sábado? (Will you come to my party on Saturday?)

¿Qué haremos si llueve? (What will we do if it rains?)

No podré asistir a la reunión mañana. (I won't be able to attend the meeting tomorrow.)

¿Quién será esa persona? No la reconozco. (Who could that person be? I don't recognize them.)

Note: Spanish also uses the periphrastic future construction "ir a + infinitive" to express future actions, especially those in the near future or plans:

Voy a estudiar esta noche. (I am going to study tonight.)

This is similar to the English "going to" future form.

Relative Pronouns "que" and "quien"

Relative pronouns connect a dependent clause to a main clause, providing additional information about a noun or pronoun. The most common relative pronouns in Spanish are "que" and "quien/quienes."

"Que" (that, which, who)

"Que" is the most versatile relative pronoun in Spanish and can refer to people, animals, or things. It's used much more frequently than "quien."

El libro que estoy leyendo es muy interesante. (The book [that] I am reading is very interesting.)

La chica que conocí ayer es española. (The girl [who/that] I met yesterday is Spanish.)

Me gusta la casa que compraron. (I like the house [that] they bought.)

Los zapatos que me regalaste son muy cómodos. (The shoes [that] you gave me are very comfortable.)

Note: Unlike English, the relative pronoun "que" cannot be omitted in Spanish. While in English you can say "The book I am reading," in Spanish you must include "que": "El libro que estoy leyendo."

"Quien/Quienes" (who, whom)

"Quien" (singular) and "quienes" (plural) are used only to refer to people. They're most commonly used after prepositions or when clarity is needed to distinguish between potential antecedents.

La profesora con quien hablé ayer es muy amable. (The teacher with whom I spoke yesterday is very kind.)

Los estudiantes a quienes enseño son de diferentes países. (The students whom I teach are from different countries.)

Mi vecino, quien es médico, me ayudó cuando estaba enfermo. (My neighbor, who is a doctor, helped me when I was sick.)

Comparison between "que" and "quien"

Feature Que Quien/Quienes
Can refer to people Yes Yes (only people)
Can refer to things Yes No
Can be used after prepositions Yes (but "el que/la que" is often preferred) Yes (common usage)
Changes form for plural No (always "que") Yes (quien → quienes)

Examples showing the difference:

La mujer que vino ayer es mi tía. (The woman who came yesterday is my aunt.)

La mujer con quien hablé ayer es mi tía. (The woman with whom I spoke yesterday is my aunt.)

Las personas que conocimos en el viaje eran muy amables. (The people whom we met on the trip were very kind.)

Las personas con quienes viajamos eran muy amables. (The people with whom we traveled were very kind.)

Note: In formal Spanish, you might also encounter "el cual/la cual/los cuales/las cuales" as relative pronouns. These forms agree in gender and number with the antecedent and are often used after prepositions or when more clarity is needed.

Reciprocal Verbs

Reciprocal verbs express actions that people perform mutually or to each other. In Spanish, they use reflexive pronouns (me, te, se, nos, os, se) but describe actions done to each other rather than to oneself.

Formation of Reciprocal Verbs

Reciprocal constructions use reflexive pronouns with plural subjects:

Subject Reflexive Pronoun Example
nosotros/nosotras nos Nos conocemos. (We know each other.)
vosotros/vosotras os Os ayudáis. (You help each other.)
ellos/ellas/ustedes se Se quieren. (They love each other.)

Note: To clarify that an action is reciprocal (rather than reflexive), Spanish often adds phrases like "el uno al otro," "mutuamente," or "entre sí" (each other, mutually, among themselves).

Se ayudan el uno al otro. (They help each other.)

Nos escribimos cartas mutuamente. (We write letters to each other.)

Common Reciprocal Verbs

1. Conocerse (to meet/know each other)

María y Juan se conocieron en la universidad. (María and Juan met each other at the university.)

Nosotros nos conocemos desde hace muchos años. (We have known each other for many years.)

¿Dónde os conocisteis vosotros? (Where did you meet each other?)

2. Caerse bien/mal (to like/dislike each other)

Ellos se caen muy bien. (They like each other very much.)

Nosotras nos caímos mal al principio. (We disliked each other at first.)

¿Vosotros os caéis bien? (Do you like each other?)

3. Darse + noun (to give each other something)

Los novios se dieron regalos en su aniversario. (The couple gave each other gifts on their anniversary.)

Nos damos consejos cuando tenemos problemas. (We give each other advice when we have problems.)

Se dieron un abrazo al despedirse. (They gave each other a hug when saying goodbye.)

4. Hacerse + noun (to do/make something to each other)

Se hacen favores todo el tiempo. (They do favors for each other all the time.)

Nos hacemos compañía cuando estamos solos. (We keep each other company when we're alone.)

Los niños se hacían bromas pesadas. (The children played practical jokes on each other.)

Other Common Reciprocal Verbs

  • abrazarse (to hug each other)
  • amarse (to love each other)
  • ayudarse (to help each other)
  • besarse (to kiss each other)
  • llamarse (to call each other)
  • mirarse (to look at each other)
  • respetarse (to respect each other)
  • escribirse (to write to each other)
  • entenderse (to understand each other)
  • verse (to see each other)

More examples:

Nosotros nos escribimos correos electrónicos todas las semanas. (We write emails to each other every week.)

Ellos se respetan a pesar de sus diferencias. (They respect each other despite their differences.)

Los hermanos se abrazan al reencontrarse. (The siblings hug each other when they meet again.)

¿Vosotros os veis con frecuencia? (Do you see each other frequently?)

Note: Remember that while reciprocal verbs use reflexive pronouns, they specifically describe actions performed by multiple people to each other. If the meaning is ambiguous, add clarifying phrases:

Se miraron. (They looked at themselves/They looked at each other.)

Se miraron el uno al otro. (They looked at each other.)

Se miraron a sí mismos. (They looked at themselves.)
