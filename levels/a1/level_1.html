Level 1 (A1)

Present Tense (Simple Present)

The Simple Present tense in Spanish (Presente de Indicativo) is used to talk about habitual actions, general truths, and current states. Regular verbs in Spanish have predictable endings based on whether they end in -ar, -er, or -ir.

Examples of verbs:
-AR verbs: hablar (to speak), trabajar (to work), estudiar (to study)
-ER verbs: comer (to eat), beber (to drink), leer (to read)
-IR verbs: vivir (to live), escribir (to write), abrir (to open)

Subject -AR (hablar) -ER (comer) -IR (vivir)
yo (I) hablo como vivo
tú (you - informal) hablas comes vives
él/ella/usted (he/she/you - formal) habla come vive
nosotros/nosotras (we) hablamos comemos vivimos
vosotros/vosotras (you all - informal, Spain) habláis coméis vivís
ellos/ellas/ustedes (they/you all - formal) hablan comen viven

Examples:

Yo hablo español. (I speak Spanish.)

Tú comes mucha fruta. (You eat a lot of fruit.)

Ella vive en Madrid. (She lives in Madrid.)

Nosotros trabajamos juntos. (We work together.)

Vosotros aprendéis rápidamente. (You all learn quickly.)

Ellos escriben cartas. (They write letters.)

Note: In many Latin American countries, "vosotros" is not used. Instead, "ustedes" is used for both formal and informal plural "you".

Uses of "Ser" and "Estar" (To Be)

Spanish has two verbs that translate to "to be" in English: ser and estar. They are used in different situations and cannot be used interchangeably.

Conjugation of "Ser" and "Estar" in Present Tense

Subject Ser Estar
yo soy estoy
tú eres estás
él/ella/usted es está
nosotros/nosotras somos estamos
vosotros/vosotras sois estáis
ellos/ellas/ustedes son están

When to Use "Ser"

Use "ser" for:

  • Identity: Yo soy estudiante. (I am a student.)
  • Origin: Ella es de España. (She is from Spain.)
  • Nationality: Nosotros somos mexicanos. (We are Mexican.)
  • Inherent characteristics: La casa es grande. (The house is big.)
  • Time: Son las tres. (It's three o'clock.)
  • Possession: Este libro es mío. (This book is mine.)
  • Material: La mesa es de madera. (The table is made of wood.)
  • Location of events: La fiesta es en mi casa. (The party is at my house.)

When to Use "Estar"

Use "estar" for:

  • Location: Madrid está en España. (Madrid is in Spain.)
  • Temporary conditions: Yo estoy enfermo. (I am sick.)
  • Emotions/feelings: Ella está feliz. (She is happy.)
  • Progressive actions: Nosotros estamos estudiando. (We are studying.)
  • Results of actions: La ventana está rota. (The window is broken.)

Examples of Contrast:

Él es aburrido. (He is boring. - an inherent characteristic)

Él está aburrido. (He is bored. - a temporary feeling)

La sopa es caliente. (The soup is hot. - it's the type of soup that's always hot)

La sopa está caliente. (The soup is hot. - it's just been heated)

Formal vs. Informal Address ("tú" vs. "usted")

Spanish distinguishes between formal and informal ways of addressing people in the second person.

Informal Address: "tú" and "vosotros"

Use tú (singular) and vosotros (plural, Spain only) for:

  • Friends
  • Family members
  • Children
  • Peers or colleagues of similar age or status
  • Pets

Formal Address: "usted" and "ustedes"

Use usted (singular, abbreviated as Ud.) and ustedes (plural, abbreviated as Uds.) for:

  • Older people
  • Authority figures
  • People you don't know well
  • Customers or clients
  • In professional settings

Note: In most Latin American countries, "vosotros" is not used. "Ustedes" is used for both formal and informal plural "you". In Argentina and some other countries, "vos" is used instead of "tú" for the informal singular "you".

Examples:

¿Tú quieres café? (Do you want coffee? - informal)

¿Usted quiere café? (Do you want coffee? - formal)

¿De dónde sois vosotros? (Where are you all from? - informal, Spain)

¿De dónde son ustedes? (Where are you all from? - formal, or any plural "you" in Latin America)

Gender of Nouns and Adjectives

In Spanish, all nouns have a gender (masculine or feminine), and adjectives must agree in gender and number with the nouns they modify.

Masculine Nouns

General rules for identifying masculine nouns:

  • Nouns ending in -o are usually masculine: el libro (the book), el plato (the plate)
  • Nouns ending in -ma are usually masculine: el problema (the problem), el sistema (the system)
  • Days of the week, months, and numbers are masculine: el lunes (Monday), el enero (January)

Feminine Nouns

General rules for identifying feminine nouns:

  • Nouns ending in -a are usually feminine: la casa (the house), la mesa (the table)
  • Nouns ending in -dad, -tad, -ción, -sión are usually feminine: la ciudad (the city), la libertad (the freedom), la canción (the song)
  • Letters of the alphabet are feminine: la "a", la "b"

Note: There are many exceptions to these rules. For example, "el día" (the day) is masculine despite ending in -a, and "la mano" (the hand) is feminine despite ending in -o.

Adjective Agreement

Adjectives must match the gender and number of the nouns they describe:

Masculine Singular Feminine Singular Masculine Plural Feminine Plural
alto (tall) alta altos altas
inteligente (intelligent) inteligente inteligentes inteligentes

Note: Adjectives ending in -e or a consonant typically have the same form for both masculine and feminine genders but still change for number.

Examples:

El chico alto (The tall boy)

La chica alta (The tall girl)

Los libros interesantes (The interesting books)

Las películas interesantes (The interesting movies)

Question Words (palabras interrogativas)

Spanish question words are always written with an accent mark when used in a question or an exclamation.

Spanish English Example
¿Qué? What? ¿Qué hora es? (What time is it?)
¿Quién? / ¿Quiénes? Who? / Whom? ¿Quién es él? (Who is he?)
¿Dónde? Where? ¿Dónde está el baño? (Where is the bathroom?)
¿Cuándo? When? ¿Cuándo es tu cumpleaños? (When is your birthday?)
¿Por qué? Why? ¿Por qué estudias español? (Why do you study Spanish?)
¿Cómo? How? ¿Cómo te llamas? (What is your name? / How are you called?)
¿Cuál? / ¿Cuáles? Which? / What? ¿Cuál es tu color favorito? (What is your favorite color?)
¿Cuánto? / ¿Cuánta? / ¿Cuántos? / ¿Cuántas? How much? / How many? ¿Cuánto cuesta? (How much does it cost?)

Note: In Spanish, questions are always marked with an inverted question mark (¿) at the beginning and a regular question mark (?) at the end.

More examples:

¿Dónde vives? (Where do you live?)

¿Cuántos años tienes? (How old are you? / How many years do you have?)

¿Cómo estás? (How are you?)

¿Qué haces? (What are you doing?)

¿Cuándo llegas? (When do you arrive?)

¿Quiénes son ellos? (Who are they?)

¿Cuál prefieres? (Which one do you prefer?)
